HKHT GE Technical Scope — Gap Analysis
Purpose
This document covers:
Workflow-wise gaps, compression risks, and missing cross-workflow architecture, Areas requiring further production clarification.
Core Concern
The current document behaves largely like:
“a Gemini Enterprise feasibility demonstration scope” instead of: “a production-ready Hyderabad AI Revenue & Seva Growth Platform architecture.”
The workflows appear compressed into isolated AI use-cases.
But the actual HKHT vision is:
AI-first operating model.
Multi-agent execution layer.
Unified relationship intelligence.
Cross-workflow orchestration.
Workflow-to-workflow intelligence transfer.
Dashboard-driven operations.
Productivity multiplication.
Relationship lifecycle intelligence.
The current scope does not yet sufficiently demonstrate:
platform-level orchestration.
unified workflow continuity.
shared data contracts.
end-to-end operational journeys.
leadership intelligence layer.
cross-workflow analytics.
operational memory layer.
workflow interoperability.
Biggest Missing Area
Cross-Workflow Continuity
The concern is lack of clarity around:
digital lead → CRM → voice follow-up → Yatra/payment → bahumana → donor follow-up
The workflows currently appear isolated. But the actual operational expectation is unified lifecycle continuity with shared relationship history.- workflow-to-workflow event transfer with centralized operational memory. Without this the platform risks becoming disconnected automations.
Workflow-wise Gaps,
WF-001 — Yatra Lifecycle
The Technical scope has shown that the system can help staff manage Yatra operations. But the bigger expectation is that the system should remember the full journey of a person.
For example, suppose one person first sees a digital ad, then fills a Yatra form, then receives a call, then pays, then joins the Yatra, then receives prasadam, and later becomes a donor. The system should not treat these as separate events. It should know that this is the same person moving through one journey.
Right now, it is not clear whether the system will connect all these steps together.
What we still need is:
The system should remember how a lead became a yatri.
The CRM should show the full Yatra journey of each yatri.
Payment status should be connected to the yatri record.
After the Yatra, the system should help convert the yatri into a donor or long-term supporter.
The system should remember the yatri’s past interest, calls, payments, family details, and future opportunities.
Staff should get one dashboard that shows Yatra registrations, payments, pending follow-ups, issues, and donor opportunities.
The system should help predict whether a Yatra will fill or needs more marketing.
Campaign source should be remembered so we know which marketing effort brought the yatri.
Follow-up should happen automatically when the yatri reaches an important stage.

How does CRM continuity persist from lead to yatri?
What IDs persist across the journey?
What events are written back to CRM?
How does Yatra lifecycle generate donor intelligence?
How are follow-up workflows triggered?
What dashboards exist for operational visibility?

The Technical scope has covered how call transcripts can be read and summarized. That is useful. But the bigger need is that every call should become part of the person’s relationship history.
For example, if a person says on a call, “I am interested in Kailash Yatra but need time,” the system should remember this, create a follow-up task, send the right WhatsApp message, and show the person as a warm Yatra lead.
Right now, it is not fully clear whether the system will do this full chain.
What we still need is:
Each call should become part of the person’s CRM timeline.
The system should remember what was discussed in past calls.
Important signals should be captured, such as donor interest, Yatra interest, payment hesitation, or complaint.
A hot lead should be prioritized above a casual lead.
After a call, the system should trigger the next action: WhatsApp, callback, human follow-up, or escalation.
Supervisors should see which calls need attention.
Failed calls, missed calls, and unresolved calls should not disappear.
Campaign source should remain connected to the call, so we know which campaign created the enquiry.
The system should show whether the relationship is new, warm, interested, pending, converted, or needs senior attention.
The system should also capture other nuance which may be important later,  if he is a frequent traveler, if he has association with ISKCON Before, if he has beem contributing to any particular cause,  What are his particular interests,  Does he mention or reference somebody during the conversation,  Any information about his business or Activities in Personal Life reveled
Enhancement: Comprehensive Data & Intelligence Capture Specification
To transform standard voice transcripts into actionable CRM intelligence, the system must parse every call and map data into distinct entity layers. This structure covers specific use cases across Yatra registrations, donation pipelines, program invites, and general follow-ups.
1. Relationship Nuance & Contextual Entities
Affiliation & Legacy Context: Automatically capture historical or active associations with spiritual centers, specific temples, mentors, or programs attended.
Behavioral Dynamics & Lifestyle Signals: Tag professional background details, business domains, travel frequencies, and personal lifestyle indicators mentioned naturally during conversation.
Philanthropic History: Map historical focus areas of giving (e.g., Anna-daan, education, construction) to align future outreach with donor passions.
The Relationship Graph: Extract names, referrals, and mutual connections mentioned during calls to automatically link profiles within the CRM.
2. Yatra Registration & Logistical Entities
Accommodation Preferences: Track specific room-sharing requests, family configurations, or physical mobility requirements (e.g., ground floor rooms, minimal stairs).
Dietary & Health Profiles: Extract strict medical allergies, chronic health conditions (essential for high-altitude Yatras), and calendar-based fasting preferences (e.g., Ekadashi tracking).
Logistical Timelines: Map arrival/departure windows, flight/train numbers, and required local transit arrangements to sync directly with ground operations.
3. Donation & Philanthropic Lead Entities
Transaction Channel Preferences: Note preferred payment modes (e.g., UPI, Net Banking, International Wire for NRI donors) to customize downstream link generation.
Tax Compliance Signalling: Explicitly flag requirements for specialized tax exemption receipts (e.g., 80G or international equivalents).
Commitment Windows: Capture ideal giving cycles, pledge fulfillment timelines, or meaningful dates (e.g., birthdays, anniversaries) for future touchpoints.
Recognition & Anonymity Settings: Track preferences regarding public donor boards, temple plaques, or festival publications.
4. Program Invites & Engagement Metadata
Headcount Matrix: Parse explicit attendee breakdowns (Adults vs. Children) to accurately predict venue capacities and catering needs.
RSVP Confidence Scoring: Assign sentiment-based confirmation values (Confirmed, Tentative - Callback Needed, Interested - Traveling) to drive automated reminder flows.
5. Operational System Metadata
Call Disposition Tags: Maintain rigid, automated system markers for instant pipeline routing (e.g., Warm Yatra Lead, Payment Hesitation, Complaint Escalation, DND Request).
Language & Dialect Mapping: Automatically lock in the contact's preferred conversational language based on call performance to route future human or AI outreach perfectly.

Cross-Workflow Areas Not Clearly Covered
1. Unified Relationship Intelligence
In simple words, the system should know the full story of each person.
For example, one person may first come through a Facebook ad, then ask a question on WhatsApp, then get a call from Blue Machines Voice Agent, then register for a Yatra, then make a donation, then receive bahumana, and later attend a community program.
The system should not treat this person as five different records. It should know: “This is the same person, and this is their full relationship journey with HKHT.”
Right now, it is not clear whether the GE scope will create this full relationship memory.
What needs clarity:
Will the system show the full journey of one person?
Will it show family links, donor links, yatri links, and community links?
Will it remember all past calls, messages, donations, events, and Yatras?
Will one workflow be able to use information from another workflow?
Without this, every team will know only one small part of the person’s story. Fundraisers, Yatra teams, outreach teams, and communication teams may all work separately without full context.
2. Shared ID Architecture
In simple words, every person and every important activity needs a fixed ID.
One Contact ID for the person.
One Donor ID if the person donates.
One Yatri ID if the person joins a Yatra.
One Campaign ID if the person came through an ad.
One Payment ID if the person paid.
One Task ID if follow-up is assigned.
Right now, it is not clear whether all workflows will use the same ID system.
What needs clarity:
Which IDs will be created?
Which system will create them?
Which ID is the main ID?
How will duplicate records be avoided?
How will one workflow recognize the same person from another workflow?
Without shared IDs, dashboards will show wrong numbers, donors may be duplicated, yatris may not connect to donation history, and campaign results may not be trusted.
3. Unified Dashboard Architecture
In simple words, leadership should not have to open thirteen different dashboards to understand what is happening.
There should be one main leadership view showing the most important things:
How many leads came?
How many became yatris?
How many paid?
How many donors need follow-up?
Which campaign is working?
Which payment or receipt is stuck?
Which tasks are delayed?
Which owner needs attention?
Operational dashboard tells us whether the organization is getting results.
Right now, it is not clear about real leadership-level operational dashboard.
What needs clarity:
Will there be one combined leadership dashboard?
What KPIs will it show?
Can it drills down to workflow, owner, donor, yatri, payment, or task?
How often will it refresh?
Which system will feed the dashboard?
Without one leadership dashboard, the team may still depend on manual follow-ups, WhatsApp updates, and repeated status asking.
4. Workflow Event Bus / Trigger Layer
when something happens in one workflow, the right next action should automatically happen in another workflow.
For example:
A person fills a Yatra form → CRM record should be created.
Blue Machines finishes a call → follow-up task should be created.
Payment is completed → Yatra status should update.
Yatra is completed → donor cultivation task should start.
Bahumana is delivered → gratitude follow-up should be triggered.
Right now, it is not clear whether the Technical scope defines this trigger system.
What needs clarity:
Which events will trigger the next workflow?
Where will these events be stored?
Who approves automated actions?
What happens if a trigger fails?
Will the system show failed triggers on a dashboard?
Without workflow triggers, staff will still manually push work from one team to another. That means automation will remain incomplete.
5. Institutional Memory
In simple words, the organization should not forget important decisions and wisdom.
Meetings should not only create tasks. They should also preserve important knowledge.
For example, if Mukund Prabhu explains why a certain donor should be approached carefully, or why a certain Yatra needs special pricing, that knowledge should be stored in a structured way. Later, the system should help others make similar decisions using that guidance with reasoning and refrence.
Right now, it is not clear whether meeting intelligence will only create tasks or also build long-term institutional memory.
What needs clarity:
Where will important decisions be stored?
Can the system find old decisions later?
Can repeated issues be detected?
Can Mukund Prabhu’s operational judgment be converted into reusable guidance?
Can future teams learn from past meetings and decisions?
Without institutional memory, the same questions will be asked again and again, and the organization will remain dependent on a few key people.
6. Productivity Intelligence
In simple words, we should be able to measure whether the system is actually saving time and improving work.
We should know:
How many follow-ups are happening on time?
How much manual chasing reduced?
How many approvals are delayed?
How many tasks are stuck?
How many leads are leaking?
How much time Mukund Prabhu is saving?
Right now, it is not clear whether the Technical scope includes this type of productivity measurement.
What needs clarity:
What productivity numbers will be tracked?
What is the current baseline?
How will improvement be measured after 30 days?
How will delayed owners and stuck tasks be shown?
How will reduction in managerial chasing be measured?
Without measurement, we cannot prove whether the AI system is creating real benefit or only creating new tools.
7. KCKE + Media AI Interface
the Krishna Consious knowledge system and media creation system should connect properly with the workflows.
For example, if a donor message or Yatra video needs a scriptural reference, the system should take it from an approved source, not just generate something generally. If Media AI creates a video final approval status and reason and learn from it for local operations.
Right now, it is not clear how KCKE and Media AI will connect with the GE workflows.
What needs clarity:
How will approved Krishna Consious sources be used?
How will citations be stored?
How will content be reviewed before use?
How will Media AI receive approved feedback details?
How will donor narratives, campaign content, and website content use KCKE safely?
How will approved content be reused later?
Without this, content may be created quickly but may not be properly source-grounded, reviewed, reusable, or connected to campaigns and donor journeys.
